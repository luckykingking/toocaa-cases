1) TL;DR

Photo engraving is one of the most impressive things a diode laser can do — but it requires careful image preparation. The laser can only burn or not burn at each point, so you need to convert your photo into a dithered pattern (dots of varying density that simulate shading). Use a high-resolution source image (≥300 DPI), apply Floyd-Steinberg dithering, and engrave on light, tight-grained wood (maple or birch plywood). The key to great results is calibrating your power/speed with a small test area before committing to the full piece.

3) HowTo: Engrave a portrait photo on maple plywood

The complete workflow from digital photo to finished wood portrait.

StepWhat you doWhy
1Select a high-contrast portrait with clear lighting. Crop tightly to the face. Resolution: ≥300 DPI at target size.Low-contrast or backlit photos produce muddy engravings. Tight crops focus detail where it matters.
2Convert to grayscale. Increase contrast by 15–20%. Sharpen slightly (Unsharp Mask: Amount 100, Radius 1.5).Pre-processing compensates for contrast loss during the engraving process.
3Apply Floyd-Steinberg dithering in your image editor or TOOCAA Studio. Export at engrave resolution (254 DPI or 10 lines/mm).Floyd-Steinberg produces the most natural-looking dot distribution for portraits.
4Place maple plywood on honeycomb bed. Verify it's flat (no warping). Set focus. Enable air assist.Even 0.5mm of warp causes visible banding in photo engravings.
5Run a test patch: engrave a 30×30mm area (e.g., the eye region) at 40% power / 6000 mm/min.The eye region has the widest tonal range — if it looks right here, the full portrait will work.
6Adjust power/speed based on the test. Then engrave the full portrait with final settings.Calibration on a test patch prevents wasting a full board on incorrect settings.
7After engraving, lightly wipe with a dry microfiber cloth. Optionally apply a thin clear coat for protection.Avoid wet cleaning — moisture can swell the wood grain and blur fine detail.
Dithering comparison: Floyd-Steinberg = most photorealistic. Jarvis = smoother, slightly less detail. Ordered/halftone = artistic dot pattern. Try all three on a test image to see which you prefer. Settings Test CSV

4) Common questions

From portrait engravers and photo gift makers.

What image resolution do I need for photo engraving?

Start with at least 300 DPI at the target engraving size. Higher is better — you can always downsample. Avoid upscaling small images as this introduces blur that ruins quality.

What is dithering and which method should I use?

Dithering converts grayscale into dot patterns the laser can reproduce. Floyd-Steinberg produces the most photorealistic results. Jarvis is smoother but less detailed. Ordered/halftone creates a distinctive artistic pattern. Try all three on a test.

Which wood is best for photo engraving?

Maple and birch plywood are the top choices: light color provides high contrast, and tight grain doesn't interfere with photo detail. Avoid open-grained woods like oak — the grain competes with the image.

My photo looks too dark / burnt

Reduce power by 5–10% and/or increase speed. Photo engraving requires lighter burns than text — you're creating subtle tonal variation. Run a test on the eye area of a portrait to calibrate before the full piece.
